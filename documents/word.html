DRAFT - Microsoft Word

The staples

Follow some of the basics:

  • Don't use images of text.
  • Don't rely soley on color.
  • Don't use fancy fonts, such as Gothic or windings.
  • Do not use images of text, unless it is something like a logo.

Techniques

Semantics

Always use the Styles tool in Word to create headings, quotes, and lists. As a bonus, if you need to change the look of your page, you can adjust the styles and everything in your page will be updated to match.

Headings

Do not use the "title" or subtitle style they don't map semantically when you export to other formats. Start the document with h1 as the first heading. Follow heading best practice (see the Structure section)

Table of Contents

With longer documents, it's good to provide a table of contents. It'll help orient users to your material, let them know what to expect, and make it easy to navigate to different sections.

Reading Level

After running spelling and grammar checks, MS Word will report your document's reading level. Directions for checking reading level.

Color Contrast

The default black text on white background has sufficient color contrast. If you start working with different color combinations, remember to check your contrast. This is something to think about when you are including charts and graphs.

Tables

Check your table settings to make sure header rows are marked. If you find yourself merging cells, you might be making a complex table. Consider if it is better to break the information into two tables.

Images

Add alt text to all your images. To open the editing panel Right click on an image and select "Properties" or select the image and use the shortcut ******. In the editing panel there are two fields: title and description. Enter the alt text in the description field. The title field is an optional field of text that will appear when someone hovers on the image.

Document Metadata

Set your title and language under Settings >

Quality Control

Accessibility Checker

Word has a built in accessibility checker that will catch things for you like missing alt text or skipped headings. Note: the tool can't check for all accesibility requirements; for example, it can't tell if the alt text is useful or not.

Older versions of Word do not have an accessibility checker.

Preparing for sharing

It's a good idea to check your document for details you do not want published. Word automatically adds information, like your account name to the author field of a documents metadata, which may or may not be appropriate for your document.
