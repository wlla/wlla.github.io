DRAFT - Text

WCAG Success Criteria

There are quite a few guidelines in WCAG to support text that is accessible in content and presentation. The Distinguishable guideline includes 8 success criteria on the visual presentation of text.

  • 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum), Level AA
  • 1.4.4 Resize Text, Level AA
  • 1.4.5 Images of Text, Level AA
  • 1.4.6 Contrast (Enhanced), Level AAA
  • 1.4.8 Visual Presentation, Level AAA
  • 1.4.9 Images of Text (No Exception), Level AAA
  • 1.4.10 Reflow, Level AA
  • 1.4.12 Text Spacing, Level AA

WCAG's Readable (3.1) Guideline includes six success criteria.

  • 3.1.1 Language of Page, Level A
  • 3.1.2 Language of Parts, Level AA
  • 3.1.3 Unusual Words, Level AAA
  • 3.1.4 Abbreviations, Level AAA
  • 3.1.5 Reading Level, Level AAA
  • 3.1.6 Pronunciation, Level AAA

Capitalization

Generally, people read the shapes of words rather than the individual letters composing a work. So the outline of a word is important element of recognition. When something is written in all caps or small caps, the letters are all the same height damaging word recognition and making it more difficult to read. Writing in all caps is also the equivalent of yelling. Instead, use appropriate capitalization. There are a couple types of capitalization including: title case, camel case, and sentance case.

With title case, each word is capitalized. This is a good alternative to all/small caps for things like labels.

With camel case, words are concatenated (stuck together with no spaces); but, the start of each word is capitalized. (Camel case is basically something written in sentance case with the spaces taken out.) Camel case should be used with hashtags. Example: #RomanceReaders

Sentance case means the first word is capitalized in a line of text.

Punctuation

Use standard punctuation. Know that some punctuation is not read by certain screenreaders.

Alignment

In a standard block of text, the words are aligned along a a few axis. Alignment describes if the text creates an even line on the right, left, or both.
